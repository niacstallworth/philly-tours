On North Broad Street in 1965, civil rights lawyer Cecil B. Moore stood face-to-face with Deputy Police Commissioner Frank Rizzo during protests outside Girard College. The confrontation wasn't just about integrating a segregated school—it was about who had the power to challenge police authority in Philadelphia, and who would pay the price for doing it.

Sixty years later, <cite index="21-1,21-2">Philadelphia's Citizens Police Oversight Commission received its largest budget increase ever in 2026: $4.2 million, up $1.2 million from the previous year, with plans to hire 13 new staff members</cite> to expand investigations, audits, and community outreach. Moore, who died in 1979, wouldn't recognize the bureaucratic language of modern oversight. But he'd understand the fundamental question at stake: can civilian authority hold police power accountable?

## The Police Brutality Lawyer

Cecil Bassett Moore came to Philadelphia in 1947 as a Marine Corps sergeant who'd served in World War II. <cite index="11-10,11-11">He earned a reputation as a no-nonsense lawyer who fought on behalf of his mostly poor, African-American clients concentrated in North Philadelphia. His cases often concerned police brutality, which brought him into conflict with the police commander and later police chief, Frank Rizzo.</cite>

Moore's legal practice was built on the everyday reality of policing in Black Philadelphia. <cite index="14-1,14-27">After receiving his law degree in 1953, Moore established a reputation as a skilled defense attorney, as well as a militant leader in Philadelphia's civil rights movement.</cite> But it was his willingness to directly confront police authority that made him a target—and a symbol.

<cite index="13-9,13-10">His activism brought him into regular confrontation with then-Deputy Commissioner of the Philadelphia Police, Frank Rizzo, who led the police response to the protests. The Girard College protests cemented Moore's role as the leader of Philadelphia's increasingly militant civil rights movement.</cite> Moore understood that civil rights without police accountability was an empty promise.

## The Modern Oversight Challenge

<cite index="4-11,4-12,4-22,4-23">Since Mayor Parker took over last year, she has kept police funding levels roughly consistent. This year's proposed $872 million is slightly less than the $877 million that the city allocated to the department last year. But the police department is still drastically understaffed. As of late December, more than 1,500 positions that had been budgeted for were vacant</cite>, even as oversight demands have grown.

The Citizens Police Oversight Commission expansion comes at a moment when <cite index="3-6">Philadelphia has seen its largest ever drop in violent crime over the past 2.5 years, despite the PD having over 1,000 vacancies.</cite> But critics argue that <cite index="3-7,3-8">despite the constant decline in people employed by the PPD, the proposed number of positions and the actual amount of our tax dollars compensating the police goes up every year. One of the major reasons for this is overtime spending—in 2024, $195 million (22.4% of total budget) was allotted to overtime.</cite>

<cite index="21-13,21-14">The Citizens Police Oversight Commission (CPOC) is a model among oversight agencies across the country. The Commission's work has already made historic strides increasing the quality of police misconduct investigations, enhancing transparency and accountability of policing, and improving the relationship between the community and the police department.</cite>

## From Street Protests to Budget Line Items

Moore's confrontational style would seem foreign in today's world of budget hearings and commission testimony. <cite index="14-2,14-28">With his brash, often confrontational manner of speaking, gravelly voice, ubiquitous cigars, penchant for bourbon whiskey, and flashy suits, Moore cut a one-of-a-kind profile.</cite> He said what many people thought but few dared say out loud.

The modern oversight commission operates in bureaucratic language Moore would have scorned. But the core mission connects: <cite index="21-6,21-7,21-10,21-11">The Citizens Police Oversight Commission (CPOC) is the official civilian oversight agency of the Philadelphia Police Department (PPD). CPOC's responsibilities include police reform, investigative efforts, data reporting, and the pursuit of accountability, transparency, and legitimacy of PPD. The Commission works to increase the transparency and accountability of PPD, improve police conduct, enhance the quality of internal investigations, and improve the relationship between the community and PPD.</cite>

<cite index="22-4,22-5">Under the leadership of Executive Director Tonya McClary, Esq., CPOC has taken major steps to strengthen its current oversight functions, increase engagement with the public, and to continue to advocate for systemic reforms. "This past year has been one of foundational growth, bold initiatives, and a steadfast commitment to the residents of Philadelphia," said McClary.</cite>

## The Unfinished Work

Moore understood that police accountability wasn't just about individual bad actors—it was about systemic power. <cite index="11-15">He was a champion of a wide range of causes central to the Civil Rights Movement, including integration of schools and trade unions, police brutality, and increased political and economic representation for poor African Americans.</cite> His approach was confrontational because he believed the system wouldn't change through polite requests.

Today's oversight commission represents a different approach to the same fundamental problem. <cite index="21-4">This funding will be used to hire 13 new staff: 3 in our Investigations Division, 2 in our Auditing, Policy, and Research Division; 3 for the Outreach Division; 2 for a new Complaint Intake Division, and an additional Deputy Executive Director, a Staff Attorney, and an Executive Assistant</cite>—institutional infrastructure Moore never had access to.

But questions remain about whether institutional solutions can address the power dynamics Moore confronted directly in the streets. <cite index="24-1">Activists demand $1.35M to fully fund the Citizens Police Oversight Commission (CPOC) to perform independent investigations and produce an annual report on the PPD's use of technology</cite>, suggesting even the expanded budget may not be enough.

## What Moore Would Ask Now

If Cecil B. Moore walked into a 2026 budget hearing, he'd probably ask the same questions he asked in 1965: Who has real power to change police behavior? What happens when oversight recommendations get ignored? And whose voices actually get heard when decisions get made?

<cite index="22-7">Looking ahead to 2026, CPOC is poised to build on its momentum through strategic staffing expansion, enhanced data reporting capabilities, and the celebration of a significant milestone — five years of impactful work.</cite> Five years is longer than Moore served as NAACP president, but shorter than the time it took to desegregate Girard College.

Moore's legacy lives on Cecil B. Moore Avenue, the North Philadelphia corridor renamed in his honor in 1987. But his deeper legacy lives in the ongoing work of holding power accountable. <cite index="17-30">He once said, "After nine years in the Marine Corps, I don't intend to take another order from any son of a bitch that walks."</cite> The Citizens Police Oversight Commission, with its expanded budget and institutional authority, represents a more diplomatic version of the same principle: civilian oversight of police power.

The question Moore would ask today is whether institutional oversight can accomplish what street protests couldn't—or whether both are necessary parts of an unfinished fight for accountability.
